Government agencies, whether they operate at the local level or the federal level, must have methods in place to create rules and enforce them. Such rules, regulations, and their enforcement are included under the umbrella of administrative law. Administrative law does not refer to a single type of law; rather, it refers to the general bodies of law that these agencies create and enforce. These regulations cover a wide array of topics and legal issues.

Administrative laws are enforced through administrative courts. For example, if you are injured in your place of employment, you may need a hearing with your local workers’ compensation commission. If an oil company spills waste into the ocean, its representatives may have to go before the Environmental Protection Agency. For work discrimination and harassment claims, employees and employers will have to argue their positions before the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission. Many of these agencies deal with highly specialized laws, and it is wise to have legal representation before proceeding in these tribunals.

Each of these agencies has a distinct process it uses to determine whether claims are meritorious. Although these procedural methods vary between government agencies, each must grant individuals that seek relief due process. Due process refers to the legal rights an individual has during adjudication. These rights may not be violated by government agencies. For example, if an individual receives social security benefits, and the Social Security Administration decides to stop those benefits, the individual has 60 days to appeal that decision. The agency cannot simply stop the benefits without giving the recipient the opportunity to question that decision.

In many cases, even if an individual is unhappy with the results of the administrative hearing, he or she may appeal that decision to a higher court. For example, administrative decisions are often appealable to state circuit courts. The individual, now called the appellant, must show how the administrative court erred in reaching its decision. Since appeals require complex analysis of the law, as well as a careful review of the facts of the case, it is advisable to seek competent counsel for representation.
